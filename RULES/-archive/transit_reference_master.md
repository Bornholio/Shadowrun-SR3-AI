## Section 1 — Source Methodology

### Geographic Foundation

Real-world Seattle geography is the skeleton. The SR3 overlay is the flesh. Where they conflict, SR3 canon wins. Where SR3 is silent, real-world geography extrapolated to 2058 fills the gap.

The real-world S Line (Sounder commuter rail) provided the station sequence and approximate travel times for the south corridor. In SR3 2058 this has been extrapolated from a weekday-only commuter service to a mixed-use rail line running more frequent service — consistent with SR3's implied functional metro infrastructure.

### USGS Lahar Data Integration

USGS hazard mapping and the community exposure publication for Mount Rainier confirmed the real-world lahar inundation zones. Key communities in the mapped hazard zones relevant to the campaign corridor:

- Orting — most severely exposed; surrounded on three sides by lahar paths; essentially no evacuation margin
- Sumner — in the inundation zone for both the Puyallup River and White River paths simultaneously; the confluence point
- Puyallup City — in the mapped zone
- Auburn — White River lahar path reaches this area; city core survived and was rebuilt
- Kent — lower White River valley lahar fringe
- Fife / Milton — both in lahar paths (Tacoma district, C-rated; consistent)

The SR3 district file name `Carbanado` is a corruption of real-world Carbonado, which sits in the Carbon River valley — itself a primary Rainier lahar corridor. This is consistent with SR3's pattern of corrupting real place names and confirms the E-rating and transit-dead status for that area.

### SR3 Canon District Files

All security ratings, demographics, economics, gang presence, and corporate facilities are drawn directly from the campaign MAPS/ files, which in turn source from New Seattle (2050 data) and the SR3 sourcebook (2060 data). Where the two editions conflict, the conflict is noted in the district files and flagged here where transit-relevant.

Kent appears in three district files with different ratings (A in Downtown, A in Tacoma, B in Renton). The station applies the highest applicable rating in practice — A — as the station is a maintained facility in the boundary zone.

### SR4+ Contamination Check

No SR4+ terminology has been imported. There are no gridguides, commlinks, AR overlays, or data throughput mechanics in this document. All communication infrastructure references use SR3 terms (LTG, radio, telephone). The transit network described is SR3-canonical: rail, bus, monorail, ferry, private vehicle, smuggler routes.

## Section 2 — Volcanic Event Canon (The Cascade Catastrophe, 2017)

This is the single most important geographic modifier in the entire campaign area. Three volcanoes erupted in close sequence during the same period as the goblinization wave. The combined effect on south Seattle infrastructure is permanent and ongoing in 2058.

### Mt. Rainier (Primary — Most Severe)

Dominant geographic feature of the entire south corridor. The 2017 eruption coincided with the goblinization wave, compounding the social chaos of the period with immediate physical catastrophe.

Effect radius: catastrophic within approximately 30km, severe to approximately 80km. The campaign's primary area of operations — Auburn, Sumner, Puyallup — falls within the severe zone.

Ash fall was persistent and accumulated. Sumner, Auburn, Kent, Puyallup, and Tacoma all received significant ashfall. By 2058 active ashfall has long ceased, but the accumulated material and its effects on infrastructure are permanent. Concrete shows characteristic grey-brown staining and accelerated spalling where ash was not cleaned promptly. Steel components in unprotected structures show elevated corrosion. Drainage systems in the Sumner/Puyallup zone are partially blocked — flooding risk during rain events is higher than pre-2017. Forest is pressing hard against weakened rights-of-way and urban edges south of Auburn.

Lahars followed the White River and Puyallup River valleys. Key effects:

`White River corridor (Auburn/Sumner)` — The flood plain is wider and less predictable than pre-2017. Lahar deposits changed the channel geometry. The original rail crossing was in a different location; the post-2017 rebuild crossed at a new point with a higher embankment approach. The visual difference is obvious: new concrete, different bridge profile, no accumulated weathering. Speed restriction on this crossing is a practical safety measure, not bureaucratic caution.

`Puyallup River corridor (Sumner/Puyallup)` — The river shifted its channel slightly after the 2017 event. The original rail bridge was compromised. The bypass route adds approximately 800 meters to the rail distance and crosses at a point with lower natural bank stability. The bypass bridge is rated for passenger loads but not heavy freight — freight is rerouted, contributing to congestion in the Sumner area.

`Carbon River corridor (Orting direction)` — Primary lahar path toward Orting. Not rebuilt. Orting (Z-rated in SR3) sits at the base of this corridor, surrounded on three sides by inundation paths. No maintained transit reaches Orting. The SR3 district name `Carbanado` is a corruption of Carbonado, which sits at the upper end of this same corridor — confirmed by geography and consistent with the E-rating in the Puyallup district file.

`Sumner confluence` — Sumner sits where the White River and Puyallup River lahar paths converge. Both valleys were inundated in 2017. This makes Sumner the most geologically exposed functional transit point on the entire south rail line. The infrastructure reflects this: patched roof sections, one unreplaced platform edge, visible repair work throughout.

Infrastructure damage summary: Roads, rail bridges, and flood control systems were damaged in 2017. Repair was partial and ongoing. Some routes never reopened. The south corridor (Auburn → Tacoma) took the worst of it.

Security implication: Areas with heavy ash damage trend toward lower security ratings. Maintaining patrol infrastructure in the degraded zone requires more resources for equivalent coverage. Lone Star allocates resources by contract value. The result is a clear gradient — AA in Auburn's city core, B–C at the Sumner fringe, C and token at Puyallup City, nothing beyond.

### Mt. St. Helens (Secondary — Significant)

Already had precedent from the 1980 eruption, which was within living memory in 2058. The 2017 eruption was southwest of the Puyallup corridor. Ash drift was primarily east and southeast — less direct impact on Seattle proper than Rainier.

SR3 canon note: Mt. St. Helens is Althain in SR3 lore. Site of significance to the Great Ghost Dance. This is a GM mystery thread and must not surface in player-facing content. Geographic and volcanic effects only are included here.

Effect on campaign area: moderate. Puyallup Barrens is partially shaped by this event alongside Rainier's effects. Not a primary transit factor.

### Mt. Hood (Tertiary — Distal)

Oregon, south of the Columbia. The 2017 eruption primarily affected the Portland/Columbia corridor. Indirect effects on Seattle: refugee pressure, trade route disruption, Columbia crossing infrastructure damaged and partially restored by 2058. Minimal direct effect on the campaign area. Noted for completeness.

## Section 3 — South Rail Corridor (Full Detail)

### Background and Character

The south rail corridor is the spine of the campaign's primary travel route. It follows the pre-Awakening commuter rail alignment from King Street Station in Downtown Seattle to Lakewood south of Tacoma. In 2058 it operates as a mixed-use line — commuter, freight, and irregular passenger service. It is not a polished metro system. Trains run more frequently than the old commuter schedule but less reliably. The farther south you go, the less reliable the schedule becomes.

The corridor carries its history visibly. North of Kent: maintained, corporate-investment infrastructure. Kent to Auburn: the ash fringe begins, older concrete shows degradation, but the stations are functional and staffed. Auburn to Puyallup: each station is progressively more improvised, more patched, more watched-over by fewer people. South of Puyallup City: the passenger rail fiction ends. What remains is freight service on repaired track, and even that is irregular.

#### King Street Station — Downtown Seattle

| Security Rating | A |

| SIN Risk | High |

King Street Station is the southern anchor of the downtown transit core and the northern terminus of the south rail corridor. The station sits in the International District at A-rated security — professionally maintained, Lone Star-staffed, but not the fortress environment of an AA or AAA enclave. Union Station (the adjacent historical structure) serves as an administrative and ticketing annex in the SR3 era.

Lone Star maintains a permanent armed detail — typically 4–6 officers visible on the platforms, more on call. SIN gates are functional at all fare entry points. During peak commute hours the gates process high volume and enforcement is somewhat rote; individual scrutiny is lower. During off-peak hours the same number of officers watch far fewer people — scrutiny is higher. This is the single most important SIN exposure point on the south corridor.

Credstick transit passes (PugetPass) are the standard payment method. A pass purchased with a clean SIN creates a transaction record. Kiosk cash purchases are possible — the kiosks are monitored and logged, but enforcement on the card itself is inconsistent. The cleanest approach for runners is either a solid fake SIN or using bus or ferry alternatives that avoid the terminal gates entirely.

The International District outside is A-rated but borders B and C zones within a few blocks. Exiting the station puts a runner into normal urban foot traffic almost immediately.

Hub connections: South Corridor (Auburn/Tacoma/Lakewood), North Corridor (Tukwila/Everett), Eastside Line (Bellevue/Renton/Kirkland), Monorail (nearby), Bus grid (multiple routes), Waterfront Ferry Terminal (10–15 min surface transit).

Sumner is McHugh's vicinity and the most geologically significant station on the line. The station structure shows this plainly: stained and spalled older concrete throughout, a partially replaced roof section, one platform edge that was never fully restored after the 2017 flooding, improvised drainage that handles heavy rain events poorly.

Security is B–C transitional. LS runs scheduled patrols but does not maintain a staffed presence. A runner arriving at off-hours is not remarkable — the station sees enough irregular traffic from the agricultural and light industrial fringe to absorb unfamiliar faces.

The surrounding area is transitional: legitimate operations mixed with abandoned properties, encroaching forest, and the beginning of the true ash zone fringe. The Puyallup River bypass bridge is nearby — lower spec, visibly different construction from the original alignment.

## Section 5 — Other Networks (Full Detail)

### Monorail

Operates on an elevated loop connecting Downtown (Westlake Center area) to Seattle Center. Approximately 2 km. Fast, climate-controlled, tourist/corporate clientele. Security is moderate: SIN expected at boarding, but tourist volume means enforcement is procedural rather than investigative. Not useful for long-distance transit. Useful for rapid movement within the downtown core without hitting street-level checkpoints. The monorail does not extend to residential or industrial zones — it is a downtown core tool only.

### Bus Grid

The most operationally useful network for runners who need to avoid SIN infrastructure. Buses cover the entire sprawl including C-rated and some D-rated zones where rail does not reach. Service quality degrades with security rating — downtown buses run on schedule; Puyallup fringe buses run when they run.

No systematic SIN gates at stops. Credstick fare or nuyen coin accepted. LS sweeps on specific routes occur but are rare and unpredictable. In E and Z zones formal bus service does not exist; informal transport fills the gap on a cash-only basis.

The bus grid is the primary transit option for residents who cannot afford or will not use rail — which in C and D zones is a significant portion of the population. This means runners using bus routes blend into normal working-class and SINless traffic rather than the more corporate-skewed rail passenger profile.

Key bus connections to south corridor: Auburn Station bus routes east into Covington, Maple Valley, and the B-rated residential belt. Routes north from Auburn into Renton. Puyallup City bus connections into Tarislar (surface route, not formal schedule).

### Ferry — Puget Sound Crossings

| Route | From | To | Security | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Bremerton | Seattle Waterfront | Bremerton, Kitsap | Moderate–High | UCAS customs presence; SIN checked at boarding |
| Bainbridge Island | Seattle Waterfront | Bainbridge | Moderate | Residential island; lighter traffic than Bremerton |
| Vashon Island | Fauntleroy (West Seattle) | Vashon | Low–Moderate | Rural island; minimal systematic checking; smuggler-adjacent |
| Victoria (BC) | Seattle Waterfront | Victoria, Salish-Shidhe | High | International crossing — SIN and border check; NAN territory protocols |

Water routes bypass all land-based checkpoints. The Bremerton and Victoria routes have the most consistent customs presence. Vashon is the lightest — the island has limited through-traffic and customs presence is minimal. Runners moving contraband or traveling without clean SINs prefer Vashon or informal watercraft over the named routes.

The waterfront terminal is not integrated into the MTA rail fare system — separate boarding and separate credstick transaction. This is a useful break in the paper trail for runners who want to switch transit modes without creating a linked record.

### Private Vehicle

Pervasive throughout the sprawl. Gridlock in the downtown core during commute hours. Freer in the south corridor where road density is lower. Standard for corporate employees and anyone who can afford fuel, insurance, and maintenance.

Security implications vary sharply by zone. In AA and AAA zones, vehicle checkpoints and plate scanning are standard. In C zones, LS may run roadblocks occasionally but patrols are light. In E and Z zones, the only vehicle risk is from other people, not law enforcement.

The lahar-modified terrain in the Auburn–Sumner–Puyallup corridor reduced road crossing options over the White River and Puyallup River. The remaining crossings are chokepoints for vehicle movement as much as for rail.

### Smuggler Routes

Not documented here. GM controlled. The south corridor's lahar-modified terrain, abandoned rail bypasses, degraded E/Z zone infrastructure, and agricultural back roads create numerous off-grid movement options. The White River agricultural corridor and the old Highway 410 / Sumner Tapps route through the ash fringe are known to be used. Detail belongs in GM notes.

## Section 6 — Fictional Transit Services (Full Descriptions)

### Ride-Hail / Taxi

**MetroCab** — City-contracted taxi service operating in A and B zones. Licensed, SIN-linked dispatch system. Clean vehicles, uniformed drivers, credstick only. Reliable in the downtown core and established neighborhoods. Drivers will not take fares into C zones after dark without a surcharge, and many will decline entirely. Never goes south of Tukwila voluntarily. The default cab option for anyone with a clean SIN and a legitimate reason to be somewhere.

**NightRide** — Private dispatch service operating primarily in B and C zones. Cash accepted, no questions asked (unofficial policy — the company doesn't advertise this). Drivers are independent contractors, not employees: no uniform, varied vehicle quality ranging from clean late-model sedans to barely-functional beaters. Dispatch via public LTG terminal or street corner contacts who know the number. The go-to for runners who need a ride without a paper trail in the middle districts. Does not operate in E or Z zones — drivers won't go there.

**Cascade Car** — Mid-tier corporate shuttle service serving AA and A zones, corporate campuses, and the airport corridor. SIN required at booking. Vehicles are clean late-model sedans with discreet corporate livery. Used by mid-level corp employees, contractors, and visitors. Useless for runners without a very solid cover identity; excellent cover if impersonating a corporate employee in a managed zone.

**Ash Road Transit** — Informal ride service operating in the Auburn/Sumner/Kent corridor specifically. Cash only, negotiated fare, no LTG dispatch — you find them by knowing where to look (certain corners in Auburn's working-class neighborhoods, the parking area outside the Sumner station during off-hours). Drivers know the lahar zone back roads intimately. Some are legitimate agricultural workers supplementing their income; some are connected to the smuggling networks that use the same roads. The line between the two categories is not always clear. Useful for movement through the ash fringe that doesn't go through the rail checkpoints.

### Courier / Package Delivery

**Federated Parcel Systems (FPS)** — The sprawl's dominant licensed courier and freight company. Corporate-affiliated, full SIN verification for sender and recipient, complete tracking from pickup to delivery. Operates in A zones and above; will not deliver to C or below. Used by corporations, law firms, hospitals, and anyone who needs their package to visibly exist in the legitimate world. Runners use FPS when they want something to look legitimate — the package is real, the sender SIN is real, and the recipient is whoever the runner wants it to be (with the right SIN). The tracking is also the vulnerability: FPS records can be subpoenaed and are stored by the company.

**Quicksilver Courier** — Licensed same-day messenger service, downtown and Eastside focus. Bicycle and light motorcycle couriers in high-visibility gear. SIN required to send. Delivery to recipient name only — no address verification on the receiving end. This gap is narrow but exploitable: a runner can receive a package at a location they don't own, using a name that's only loosely tied to a SIN, with plausible deniability about the contents.

**Grey Packet** — Unlicensed courier network operating across B through E zones. No SIN, no tracking, no questions about contents. Payment in nuyen at handoff — half on pickup, half on confirmed delivery via a third-party signal. Packages are handled by a chain of unknown intermediaries; the entry contact doesn't know who delivers and the delivery person doesn't know who sent. Reliable for the right cargo if you know the entry contact, which requires the right introductions. Response time is measured in hours, not minutes. Will not operate in Z zones.

**Cascade Freight** — Regional truck freight company specializing in the south corridor: Auburn, Sumner, Tacoma, and the port. Legitimate cargo operation moving industrial goods for Federated-Boeing, Bowman Metal Works, and agricultural operations. Licensed, SIN-verified manifests, regular routes. Drivers know every road in the lahar corridor including the ones that don't appear on current maps. Some drivers supplement their income by carrying unlisted cargo for cash. The company officially does not know this happens. Plausible deniability is maintained by all parties. The best entry point is through a driver contact, not through the company.

**Deadletter** — Not a company. A loose network of runners, gangers, and SINless who move packages inside Puyallup and Redmond Barrens zones where no legitimate service operates. Payment in advance, always. No guarantees of delivery, timeline, or condition on arrival. The service is the only option in those zones, which is why it exists — not because it's good. Entry through local contacts only.

### Chartered / Specialty Transit

**Metroplex Transit Authority (MTA)** — The official government body operating the bus grid, rail stations (in partnership with corporate contractors), and the monorail. Bureaucratic and chronically underfunded relative to the size of the sprawl it serves. Political management means resources follow political priorities, not operational need. Lone Star has separate jurisdiction at MTA facilities — the two organizations share space but not authority, which creates regular low-level friction and occasional serious gaps in coverage.

**PugetPass** — The credstick-linked transit pass system for MTA bus and rail. SIN-linked on purchase at a staffed counter. Available at automated kiosks for nuyen cash with minimal identity verification — the kiosk logs the transaction including approximate location and time, but enforcement on the card itself during transit use is inconsistent. A nuyen-purchased PugetPass is not clean, but it's cleaner than a SIN-linked one for runners who need a paper trail that goes cold quickly.

**Horizon Charters** — Corporate shuttle charter service operating exclusively in AA and AAA zones. Private coaches moving corporate employees between campuses, airports, and high-security facilities. Armed private security accompanies high-value routes (Bellevue ↔ Downtown is the primary one; Renraku Arcology runs exist but are handled by Renraku's own vehicles). Not accessible to non-corporate personnel without a very solid cover identity — the passenger list is verified against corporate employee databases, not just SIN.

**Freewheel** — Unlicensed van pool service operating in B and C zones. Fixed informal routes that don't change much — the same van runs the same route at roughly the same times because the same workers need to get to the same places. Cash fare, no schedule posted anywhere official (leaves when full, not at a fixed time). Drivers are regulars; passengers become familiar faces quickly. Good for local color and understanding how working-class people move through the middle zones. Bad for operational anonymity — a new face on a regular route is noticed within two or three trips.

**River Road Runs** — Informal transit along the White River agricultural corridor between Auburn and Sumner. Trucks and vans belonging to farm operations, running on the roads that connect agricultural properties through the lahar-modified flood plain. Not an organized service — drivers take passengers to offset fuel costs. Cash only. Will take passengers to destinations that don't appear on any official route, including spots in the ash fringe that no formal transit reaches. The drivers know the terrain in a way that no map captures.

**AirMetro** — Rotorcraft charter service operating between Downtown and Eastside helipads in AA and AAA zones. Expensive (corporate expense-account pricing). Serves executives, high-value corporate visitors, and the very wealthy. SIN required at booking with verification against corporate or personal credit accounts. A runner using AirMetro is either burning a pristine high-quality cover identity or operating under circumstances unusual enough that cost and exposure are secondary concerns.

**Troll Gate Transit** — Not a formal service. A mutual aid arrangement run by members of the Puyallup City community providing irregular transport between Puyallup City and Tarislar (the elven enclave). Named informally — the route passes through an area where a troll community has established an informal checkpoint on the road. Small cash fee, mostly to cover fuel. Outsiders are tolerated but need to be vouched for by someone the driver knows. Reflects the community self-organization that exists in the absence of corporate infrastructure.

**The Milk Run** — Local slang for the irregular passenger service on the south rail corridor between Auburn and Puyallup. Not an official name; a sarcastic one that stuck. Refers to the fact that the train makes every stop, runs slow, the schedule is optimistic at best, and the cargo manifest often includes actual agricultural goods alongside passengers. Used affectionately by regulars and with resigned frustration by everyone else. Any runner who spends time in Auburn will hear this term.

## Section 7 — Volcanic Zone Transit Notes (Full Detail)

### Ash Zone Character

Persistent ashfall from Rainier affected the entire south corridor from Kent southward, with intensity increasing through Auburn, Sumner, and into Puyallup. By 2058 the active event is forty years past, but the accumulated material is a permanent feature of the landscape.

Effects on infrastructure visible in 2058:
- Concrete surfaces: grey-brown staining, accelerated spalling on uncoated or under-maintained surfaces
- Steel: elevated corrosion rates in unprotected older structures
- Drainage: systems in the Sumner/Puyallup zone are partially blocked; rain events create flooding risk that did not exist pre-2017
- Vegetation: forest pressing against weakened rights-of-way; the urban edge south of Auburn is soft — nature is winning ground from abandoned properties

These are atmospheric and descriptive details. They are not mechanical modifiers unless the GM rules otherwise, but they inform the character of every scene set in the ash zone fringe.

### Specific Zone Notes

`Orting` — Z-rated. Sits directly in the primary Rainier lahar inundation path, surrounded on three sides. The USGS data identifies Orting as the most severely exposed community in the Rainier hazard zone — essentially no evacuation margin even in the real world, let alone a world where the eruption actually happened at catastrophic scale. In SR3 2058 the town is a Z-rated dead zone. No maintained transit. Access is foot or improvised vehicle only, with the understanding that the terrain itself is hostile: lahar deposits, structural collapse, accumulated ash. SR3 does not have specific rad mechanics but the general environmental danger framework applies.

`Carbanado` — E-rated, Puyallup district. The SR3 name is a corruption of Carbonado, a real town at the upper end of the Carbon River valley — itself a primary Rainier lahar path. The Carbon River corridor connects toward Orting; both ends are dead zones. Seoulpa Ring influence is noted in the Puyallup district file, which is consistent with criminal operations taking advantage of an area where law enforcement genuinely cannot go. No transit access.

`Sumner confluence` — The most geologically exposed functional transit point on the south corridor. The station itself is partially repaired and shows its history in every surface. The confluence of the two major lahar valleys makes this the point of maximum 2017 impact that was subsequently rebuilt enough to function. It functions. It doesn't look good doing it.

`Chokepoint mechanics` — In any scenario involving a security crackdown, military action, or controlled evacuation in the Auburn–Sumner corridor, the White River and Puyallup River crossings are the natural seal points. The lahar-modified terrain reduced natural crossing options significantly. The remaining bridges are known, numbered, and coverable. A GM running a scenario where the corridor needs to be locked down should place the checkpoints at the bridges, not at the stations.

### Glow City — Redmond

`Glow City` is a Z-rated radioactive zone in the Redmond district. SR3 canon establishes it as a site of nuclear contamination from an industrial or weapons incident (the specific backstory is not detailed in core canon — treat as established fact, origin as GM flavor). In transit terms: no transit enters or exits. Anyone moving through accepts radiation exposure.

The contamination is not a hard wall. There is a gradient: elevated risk begins before the Z-rated core boundary, increasing toward the hot center. This means the fringe of Glow City is dangerous but not instantly lethal — scavengers, desperate SINless, and criminal operations dealing in exotic or illegal materials work the fringe. The Z boundary is where the risk becomes acute.

SR3 core does not have a detailed radiation exposure mechanic. GM discretion applies — reasonable approaches include: damage per hour of exposure, escalating tier system based on proximity to the center, or narrative consequence rather than mechanical damage for fringe exposure.

For transit documentation purposes: there is nothing to document. No route enters Glow City. No route exits through it. If runners go there, they go on foot or by improvised vehicle and they are on their own.
